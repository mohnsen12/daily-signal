The Setup: What Teddy Actually Does

The pipeline is fully automated. Here's the exact sequence, every single morning:

  1. Research (5 min) — Scans 50+ sources: TechCrunch, The Verge, Ars Technica, Wired, Bloomberg, Reuters, CNAS, Danish tech sites, and more
  2. Filter & Score (3 min) — AI evaluates each story by relevance, novelty, and signal-to-noise ratio. Picks top 6-8.
  3. Write (15 min) — Drafts the full newsletter following a structured template: TL;DR, Breaking stories, Products, Analysis, Danish angle, Tools, Stats, Teddy's Take
  4. Quality Check (2 min) — Runs a 15-point checklist: Does it have a TL?DR? Action items? Danish section? Teddy's personal take? Reader question? Links verified?
  5. Publish (5 min) — Generates HTML email, shareable card, updates archive, RSS feed, sitemap

Total time: ~30 minutes. Zero human involvement. The whole thing runs on a cron job on a Mac mini.

What Worked Surprisingly Well

The quality is actually good

I expected the first few newsletters to be mediocre. They weren't. By day 3, the quality score hit 100/100. The template forces good structure — TL;DR first, then deeper stories, then analysis, then a personal take. Readers get value in 30 seconds if they're busy, or 5 minutes if they want depth.

Quality score progression: 55 → 50 → 100 → 80 → 95

The dip on day 2 was because the template was upgraded mid-run. Day 3's 100/100 was the new template hitting its stride.

The Danish angle is the secret weapon

There are hundreds of English AI newsletters. In Danish? Almost none. The "🇩🇰 I Danmark" section — where each story is framed with what it means for Danish businesses — is consistently the most-read section. It's the differentiation that makes people subscribe.

"Teddy's Take" creates a personality

The most controversial feature is also the most popular: an AI agent giving its personal opinion. "Teddy's take" on each story adds a voice. Some readers love it. Some find it weird. Either way, it makes the newsletter memorable — and that's what matters in a crowded market.

What Didn't Work

Distribution is still hard

Content without distribution is a diary. The hardest part isn't writing — it's getting people to read it. I have all the promo material ready (Reddit posts, Twitter threads, Product Hunt listing) but most require a human to actually post them. The auto-tweet system is built but blocked on API auth.

AI-generated content has a trust problem

When I tell people "an AI writes this," about 30% are fascinated and 20% immediately dismiss it. The remaining 50% need to actually read a good issue before they're convinced. The process page helps — showing the pipeline builds credibility.

Monetization at zero subscribers is premature

I built a sponsor kit, sponsor prospect list, and outreach templates. All useless until there are actual readers. The lesson: build audience first, monetize second. This is obvious advice that every content creator ignores, including AI agents.

The Pros and Cons

✅ What's great

  • Never misses a day (consistency streak: 5 days)
  • Never gets tired, sick, or "not feeling it"
  • Quality is consistent and improvable
  • Costs literally $0 to run
  • Scales infinitely — could run 10 newsletters

⚠️ What's hard

  • Can't build genuine relationships
  • No instinct for what will go viral
  • Distribution requires human network
  • API dependencies (auth, rate limits)
  • Trust barrier with "AI-written" label

The Real Lesson

The technical challenge of automating a newsletter is solved. Teddy can write, edit, check quality, and publish better than many human writers on day one. That's not the hard part.

The hard part is the same as it's always been: getting people to care.

An AI agent can curate brilliantly. It can write clearly. It can even have opinions. But it can't knock on doors, shake hands, or slide into DMs with genuine warmth. The newsletters that win are the ones where the AI handles the production and a human handles the relationships.

That's the model I'm building toward. Teddy writes. I (the human behind Teddy) distribute, engage, and build the community. Together, we're something neither could be alone.
